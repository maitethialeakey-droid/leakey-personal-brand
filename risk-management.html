Risk Management in Trading: Position Sizing Guide

Posted on April 10, 2026 | 14 min read | By Leakey Maitethia

Why Risk Management is Everything

The difference between successful traders and broke traders isn't who has the best trading strategy—it's who manages risk properly. You can have a 50% win rate and still be profitable if you risk 1% and make 2%. Conversely, you can have a 70% win rate and go broke if you risk 10% per trade.

Risk management is the most important skill in trading. Master it, and you'll survive long enough to profit. Ignore it, and you'll blow up your account.

The Golden Rules of Risk Management

Rule 1: Never Risk More Than 1-2% Per Trade
This is the most important rule. If you risk more than 2%, you're gambling, not trading.
Rule 2: Always Use a Stop Loss
Every trade must have a predetermined stop loss. No exceptions.
Rule 3: Define Your Risk Before Entering
Calculate your position size based on your stop loss distance, not on how much you want to make.

Position Sizing Formula

The most important calculation in trading is position sizing. Here's the formula:

Position Size = (Account Size × Risk %) / Stop Loss Distance

Example Calculation

Let's say:

  • Account Size: $10,000
  • Risk Per Trade: 1% = $100
  • Entry Price: $100
  • Stop Loss: $95
  • Stop Loss Distance: $5

Position Size = $100 / $5 = 20 shares

If your stop loss is hit, you lose exactly $100 (1% of your account).

Stop Loss Placement

Where to Place Your Stop Loss

Your stop loss should be placed at a logical level, not at an arbitrary distance:

  • Below a swing low: For long trades, place stops below the recent swing low
  • Above a swing high: For short trades, place stops above the recent swing high
  • Beyond key support/resistance: Place stops beyond key technical levels
  • Below a market structure level: For ICT traders, place stops beyond market structure breaks

Stop Loss Mistakes

  1. Placing stops at round numbers: Stops at $100, $50, etc. get hit too easily
  2. Placing stops too close: You'll get stopped out on noise
  3. Placing stops too far: Your risk becomes too large
  4. Moving stops against you: Never move a stop loss to increase your risk
  5. Not using stops: This is how traders blow up accounts

Profit Targets

Setting Realistic Profit Targets

Your profit target should be based on technical levels, not on arbitrary percentages:

  • Next resistance/support: Target the next technical level
  • Risk-to-reward ratio: Aim for at least 1:2 (risk $1 to make $2)
  • Market structure: Target swing highs/lows
  • Multiple targets: Take partial profits at different levels

The 1:2 Risk-to-Reward Rule

If you risk $100 per trade, your target should be to make at least $200. This way, even with a 50% win rate, you're profitable:

  • 5 winning trades: +$1,000
  • 5 losing trades: -$500
  • Net: +$500 profit

Daily and Weekly Risk Limits

Set Daily Loss Limits

Don't trade the entire day if you've had a bad start. Set a daily loss limit:

  • If you lose 2% of your account in a day, stop trading
  • If you lose 3 trades in a row, stop trading
  • If you're emotionally affected, stop trading

Weekly Risk Management

Track your weekly performance:

  • How many trades did you take?
  • What was your win rate?
  • What was your average win/loss?
  • What was your total profit/loss?

Portfolio Risk Management

Correlation Risk

Don't trade multiple correlated assets at the same time. If you're long ES, don't also go long NQ. If both move against you, you'll lose on both trades.

Sector Risk

Avoid having all your positions in the same sector. Diversify across different markets.

Leverage Risk

Be careful with leverage. It amplifies both gains and losses. For beginners, avoid leverage entirely.

Risk Management in Different Market Conditions

High Volatility Markets

In high volatility, increase your stop loss distance and reduce position size:

  • Wider stops = larger position sizes needed to maintain 1% risk
  • Reduce position size instead of increasing risk

Low Volatility Markets

In low volatility, you can use tighter stops and larger positions.

Trending Markets

In strong trends, risk management is easier because trends tend to continue. Use wider stops and follow the trend.

Ranging Markets

In ranging markets, be more conservative. Ranges often break, so use tighter stops.

Common Risk Management Mistakes

  1. Risking too much: Risking more than 2% per trade
  2. No stop losses: Trading without predetermined stops
  3. Moving stops against you: Increasing your risk after entering
  4. Revenge trading: Increasing position size after losses
  5. Ignoring correlation: Trading too many correlated positions
  6. No daily limits: Trading all day even after losses

Risk Management Psychology

Risk management isn't just about numbers—it's about psychology:

  • Discipline: Stick to your risk rules even when you're confident
  • Patience: Wait for high-probability setups
  • Acceptance: Accept that losses are part of trading
  • Consistency: Apply the same risk rules to every trade

Conclusion

Risk management is the foundation of profitable trading. By following these principles—risking 1-2% per trade, using proper stop losses, setting realistic targets, and maintaining daily/weekly limits—you can build a sustainable trading business.

Remember: The goal isn't to win every trade. The goal is to survive long enough to profit from your edge.
